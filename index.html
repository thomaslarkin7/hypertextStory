Behind the Bestiary
Welcome to Behind the Bestiary, where you can learn more about medieval bestiaries and the creatures that went into developing the Fantastic Bestiary adventure.

What is a Bestiary?
A bestiary is a text or manuscript that contains descriptions about animals – real and mythical – and sometimes plants and environments, which stood as allegories for Christian moral codes. Because of this, they were used as religious texts and incorporated into sermons by the Church. Bestiaries were popular in England during the Middle Ages and the descriptions were generally accompanied by beautiful hand-drawn illustrations.



Not only are bestiaries historical religious texts, but they also serve as an interesting example of manuscript production – a bestiary would be copied by hand to create a new copy or version. Because of this version process, bestiaries are sometimes categorised as First families (earlier or first version) or Secondary families (later versions) and/or Transitional (versions that are the bridge between First and Secondary families).



What bestiary was used for 'A Fantastic Bestiary'?
The bestiary used in this Choose Your Own Adventure is an early 13th Century Latin version from central or northern England, held in the British Library’s collection, shelfmark: Royal MS 12 C. xix.

This version is related to an earlier bestiary (c. 1185) from the Worksop Priory, Nottinghamshire. It is also related to a later bestiary (c. 1250-1260) from Alnwick Castle . Because of these relationships, Royal MS 12 C. xix is considered a transitional bestiary that shows the changes or evolutions between a First (Worksop Priory) and Second (Alnwick Castle) Family bestiaries. It is also identified as missing several folios, or pages.

You can view a digitised copy of Royal MS 12 C. xix at the British Library’s Turning Pages site here .

Who wrote bestiaries?
While the specific creator of this bestiary is unknown, it is based off the Greek Physiologus and the Etymologiae of St. Isidore of Seville.

Copying or writing illuminated manuscripts (texts with illustrations) was usually undertaken by scribes and these beautiful works were created for wealthy aristocracy or the clergy - people who had the wealth to create expensive manuscripts, and could also understand latin, the language most manuscripts were written in.

Royal MS 12 C. xix, with its bright illustrations, encircled by gold shows that it would have been an expensive manuscript to make.

Other authors of medieval bestiaries that were frequently copied include Guillaume le Clerc, a Norman cleric and Old French poet, and Philip de Thaon, an Anglo-Norman poet who was used as inspiration for A Fantastic Bestiary.

What influences have bestiaries had on modern culture?
Medieval bestiaries and the creatures within them have impacted on modern popular culture greatly. Many of the mythical creatures within bestiaries have become widely popular in genres such as Fantasy fiction, television and film, to classical texts and artworks, music and the performing arts.

The Crocodile
*The crocodile is not featured as a standalone creature in Royal MS 12 C. xix, but is represented in the illustration for the Hydrus.

The crocodile is born and lives in the Nile river in Egypt and has sharp teeth and claws. While its skin is so hard that it affected by hard blows/attacks, its belly is slightly more vulnerable. Only two animals can kill a crocodile - the Sawfish (cutting the crocodiles belly) and the Hydrus (by entering the crocodile’s mouth and killing it from the inside). In many bestiaries, crocodiles are said to cry or weep after eating people.

Real or Mythical?
This creature is real!

The Basilisk

While the basilisk in modern popular culture is depicted more as a snake or serpent, in many medieval bestiaries, it is shown as a creature with the body of a rooster and the tail of a snake. In the British Library’s Royal MS 12 C. xix, there are several creatures associated with the basilisk as part of the ‘serpent’ genus, including the Regulus and Sibilis.
